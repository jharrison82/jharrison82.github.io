Account Based Marketing (ABM)

In this chapter

  • What to do instead

Once the digital marketing infrastructure is in place, some teams might be tempted to try account based marketing (ABM) — a cool-sounding buzzword.

Problems with account based marketing

The main idea behind ABM is the marketing team working with sales to identify key accounts and delivering those accounts a personalised message based around their unique "pain points" (which your product presumably helps to solve). Sounds great, right?

However, within investment management, there’s a few key issues that — in my opinion at least — render ABM essentially useless:

  • Investment managers usually offer multiple investment products
  • Investment products can be assessed without case studies or demos
  • Pain points will vary, and you won’t be able to determine them anyway

Without knowledge of an account’s specific pain points, and no appropriate content to attend to them anyway, what tends to happen with ABM programs is that marketing simply adds a logo to standardised content and sends it out via “personalised” emails, along with some generic targeted advertising.

And more broadly, the whole concept isn’t in line with the inbound marketing methodology — you’re sending out content to contacts who haven’t asked for it. When attempted by investment managers, ABM programs will inevitably run out of steam, but not without first annoying the firm’s most important audience members.

Unfortunately, these flaws are camouflaged by unnecessary complexity and buzzwords introduced by educational content about ABM, along with a lack of practical examples of how it should work in practice. Marketers without true expertise and experience may become true believers who can be sold useless sales platforms.

What to do instead

As pain points can’t be determined for specific accounts, the ABM concept can therefore be theoretically expanded into a single program that covers the firm’s entire audience — and thereby cancelling itself out.

Instead, sales can be left to focus on their most important accounts themselves — organising events, webinars, dealing with pain points or product needs as they come up in meetings — with marketing providing support via the digital marketing infrastructure that has been set up, allowing each contact to decide what they want to receive.
