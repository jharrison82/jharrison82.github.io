Once the digital marketing infrastructure is in place, some teams might be tempted to experiment with account based marketing (ABM).

What is account based marketing?

ABM is a strategy where marketing works closely with sales to identify the firm’s most important accounts and deliver them “personalised” messaging which addresses their specific “pain points” (which the firm’s products presumably help to solve).

Why investment managers don't need ABM

Unfortunately, the true utility of ABM is obscured by unnecessary complexity and buzzwords used to explain the concept, along with a dearth of practical examples of how to actually do it. Furthermore, the peculiarities of investment management pose even more issues:

  • Investment managers usually offer multiple investment products
  • Investment products can be assessed without case studies or demos
  • Pain points often can't be determined at an account level

The main difficulty — apart from justifying unsolicted communications — is coming up with content, so marketing usually end up putting a target firm’s logo on standard content and distributing it through “personalised” emails or targeted ads, which inevitably fizzles out — but not before irritating some of their most important audience members.

A use case for “restricted” ABM

A financial intermediary firm (such as a private bank or wealth manager) might have a number of an investment manager’s pooled funds on an internal recommended list (used by their client-facing advisors to construct portfolios).

Therefore, an investment manager could send periodic updates to contacts at these firms with information about the funds on their recommended list (e.g., characteristics, how they fit into portfolios, latest materials, discounted share classes, etc.).

However, although this type of communication is the only truly account-specific example I can think of, ultimately the investment manager would be communicating with individual contacts who haven’t expressed an interest. Ignoring subscription preferences for non-operational communications is not a good idea, especially as they can't be unsubscribed from.

Therefore, depending on how options for website subscriptions have been set up — if individual funds can be subscribed to, for example — it can be a good idea to restrict this type of communication to existing subscription preferences. For example, an email about funds on a recommended list can be restricted to contacts who have subscribed to any of the funds mentioned. That way, contacts still receive emails based on their individual preferences and are able to unsubscribe.

What to do instead of ABM

A better alternative to ABM is already embedded in this guide:

  • Marketing ensure website visitors can quickly find what they need
  • Website visitors subscribe to topics and receive communications that match their individual interests
  • Salespeople are sent information about the digital activity of their tracked contacts to help with targeted follow-ups, especially for contacts at key accounts
  • A restricted form of ABM is utilised when needed
  • Targeted advertising is used sparingly to help attract new audiences
