Investment managers publish content on a range of topics (including interest rates, inflation, valuations, investment principles, and portfolio holdings) in multiple formats (website articles, PDFs, videos, and podcasts).

By producing content the target audience finds valuable — without pushing products — a firm can demonstrate its expertise, establish credibility, attract new audiences, and build trust with subscribers.

This chapter shares a few ideas around best practice.

What's the best format for content?

In my experience, short-form written content (i.e., a few paragraphs and an occasional chart) is the best choice of format, because it:

  • Reflects how most website visitors consume content (i.e., short attention spans, scanning behaviour)
  • Can cover key points and link to deeper material if needed
  • Requires less resources than other formats (e.g., video production)

If your firm publishes long articles on its website — which investment managers are fond of doing — and the data is available, check if they are being read. If they aren’t, consider:

  • Replacing the full text with a short summary and making the complete article available as a downloadable PDF
  • Encouraging authors to write shorter, blog-style content instead

Engagement levels for resource-heavy formats (e.g., podcasts) should also be reviewed, as they are sometimes introduced because it’s the “done thing” rather than from audience demand. However, even with evidence of limited traction, some firms continue trying to force their success.

Categories & tagging

Each piece of content is typically categorised (either assigned to a single category and/or tagged so they can be associated with multiple) to help visitors browse the website, as well as underpinning subscription preferences.

Common examples of categories include asset classes, investment teams, topics, and firm-specific content series.

I have found asset classes to be the best fit for categorising content — they are standardised across the industry, consistent, and naturally align with investment offerings — whereas the other examples can present challenges:

  • Their meaning may be unclear to new visitors
  • Investment teams can change due to internal restructures
  • Firm-specific content series often lose momentum and become redundant

A few investment managers have an influential person or team whose content becomes incredibly popular in its own right (e.g., Bill Gross's Investment Outlooks or Howard Marks's Memos), but it's rare, can take a long time to build popularity, and adds key-person risk.

When creating a category list, the four main asset classes — (1) equity, (2) fixed income, (3) multi-asset, and (4) alternatives — are a good place to start. These can be split into sub-asset classes or by region to align with the firm’s product range, if needed.

I've found that two non-asset class categories — macro & markets and sustainable investing — can be useful additions.

Task

Create a category list for website content that aligns with the firm's range of products and strategies.

Creating a publishing process

Having an established process ensures content is published in a timely, consistent, and efficient manner. It usually involves the content marketing team sending "posting pack" emails to the digital marketing team that provide:

  • Title, tagline, summary paragraph(s), and article copy
  • Video, audio, and PDF files or links
  • Author information
  • Relevant categories and/or tags
  • Website imagery and charts

They often include pre-specified dimensions for images and character limits for copy, as well as a requirement that all content has received compliance approval before it reaches the digital marketing team.

To help with SEO, the content marketing team can reference the keyword list so they can include relevant keywords in content (however, the team should refrain from keyword stuffing and ensure the copy reads well).

If it’s not being done already, the marketing team can encourage colleagues — ideally fund managers or investment strategists — to contribute their ideas to the content marketing team, which can be refined as necessary.

Task

Ensure a publishing process is in place.
