Goals & Objectives

In this chapter
  • Introduction
  • Why objective-setting doesn’t work
  • A better approach: Focus on how you work

When developing a digital marketing strategy, it is often assumed the first step is to define a big-picture goal and break it down into measurable objectives. Apparently, this helps to provide direction and track progress.

In my view, though, it's a waste of time. Thinking up a vague goal isn’t particularly useful, and there are multiple problems with trying to set measurable objectives.

Why objective-setting doesn’t work

A common objective for investment managers is to grow assets under management (AUM) over time. They do so by generating inflows of money into products and preventing sales of existing investments (alongside any increase in value of the underlying investments).

However, as it's essentially impossible to link these flows to marketing activity, the kind of reporting leadership would like to see (marketing-attributed sales, return on investment) isn’t calculable.

Teams might be tempted to fall back on setting SMART objectives tied to more accessible marketing KPIs — leads generated, website traffic, email open rates, and so on — but I suspect very few teams actually do so.

Attempting to use marketing KPIs usually fails, for a variety of reasons. These include inherent flaws with the objectives themselves (e.g., chosen arbitrarily, can't account for simple nuances, not actionable), a need for the KPI data to be consolidated within a single system, and because eventually it's discovered that no-one in the business actually cares about seeing them.

A better approach: Focus on how you work

Instead of wasting time on setting goals and objectives, try focusing on how the team works work day-to-day. “Slow Productivity” (by Cal Newport) offers a compelling framework for this:

  1. Do fewer things
  2. Work at a natural pace
  3. Obsess over quality
