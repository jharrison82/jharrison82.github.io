Marketing Materials

In this chapter
  • Introduction
  • Collating materials
  • Setting up download tracking

The most important marketing content produced by investment managers is the range of materials for the firm’s products and strategies.

Although these materials — which are mostly PDFs — might be considered somewhat old-fashioned, in my opinion they largely deliver what the firm’s audience needs to analyse investments.

Because they are so key — as well as being used in marketing campaigns and online, salespeople share these materials with their contacts — the sales team should be given full visibility into what is available and be made aware of any downloads by their assigned contacts (who’s digital activity is being tracked by the marketing automation system).

In this chapter, we collate marketing materials, set up download tracking, and create an internal document library.

Collating materials

The first step is to determine what types of document are produced for individual products and strategies. Common examples include:

  • Factsheets — periodic updates (monthly or quarterly) on investment performance and portfolio holdings
  • Pitchbooks/presentations — in-depth documents covering investment philosophy, process, people, performance, and the investment case
  • Flyers — a short narrative document, usually around 1 or 2 pages, with key content taken from a relevant pitchbook/presentation
  • Commentaries — periodic commentary (monthly, quarterly, or annually) on performance, attribution, changes to portfolio holdings, and outlook

Most of these materials are produced on a one-to-one basis: a single document for each investment strategy or product. For pooled funds, however, some documents — such as the factsheet — are typically published separately for each share class. Document types may vary for each set of strategies and fund ranges.

Often, due to resource constraints, not every strategy or product will have the full set of documents. The next step, therefore, is to note which specific documents are available for each strategy and product.

To help, a matrix document can be created with document types in the column headers and product/strategies in the row headers, as shown in figure 1. An initial list can be created for strategies (if appropriate) and the process can then be repeated for each product range as needed.

Once all product and strategy materials have been collated, the final step is to identify and note down any other materials you think are relevant — a corporate brochure or presentation, firm factsheet, fund range brochures, regulatory documents, and so on.

Task

Collate relevant marketing materials by:

  • Listing which document types exist for products and strategies, noting if any are produced at the share class level
  • Identify which specific documents are available for each individual product and strategy (use the matrix document above if helpful)
  • Identify and list any other documents you consider relevant

If the firm publishes materials in multiple languages, these will need to be included.

Setting up download tracking

Once all materials have been identified, the next step is to make sure downloads are tracked by the marketing automation system so they can be attributed to tracked contacts. Without this step, we would only know how many times a document was downloaded via the website analytics software (e.g., Google Analytics) but wouldn’t know who downloaded them.

Note that this step can be skipped if you are hosting documents in the marketing automation system, as any downloads should already be attributed to specific tracked contacts.

Otherwise, your firm will probably be hosting documents in one of many available document management systems – some of which require a lot of unnecessary tagging or other maintenance – or even externally (for example, if another company produces and hosts factsheets).

Regardless of which system your firm uses, or if documents are stored externally, there is one simple requirement: each document should be available on a static, publicly available URL. The link should always link to the latest version of the document and not change when a new version of a document is uploaded (obviously, it should also be clear who is responsible for updating each document, to ensure the links remain up-to-date).

Then, a redirect URL needs to be created in the marketing automation system for every document. This link redirects to the original document URL and, without the downloader noticing anything different, makes the marketing automation system aware of the download. If the contact is being tracked, the download is assigned to that contact and logged on their contact record.

Additionally, marketing automation systems usually allow the redirect link to be created as a more memorable vanity URL – which is helpful if the document management system uses long, complicated links.

As an example, a factsheet for a US Equity Fund may be hosted on the following link in the firm’s document management system:

							https://documents.lam.com/api/documents/view?documentid=JHysSSjuS_22

In this case a redirect link is created in the marketing automation system, which is much more readable:

https://www2.lam.com/documents/lam-us-equity-fund-factsheet-a-acc-usd.pdf

This URL redirects to the original link in the document management system. Anyone who downloads the link will simply see the document as normal, but if they are being tracked by the marketing automation system, the download is attributed to them and logged on their contact record. Additionally, the link, if viewed in Google Analytics or any other website analytics software, is much more clearer as to what is being downloaded.

Task

Create redirect links for all marketign materials.
