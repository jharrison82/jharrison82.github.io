In this chapter, we briefly outline the typical website setup for a B2B investment manager.

Content

Most websites will consist of content grouped into four sections:

  • Products — information about available products & strategies
  • Insights — regularly published blogs, videos and webinars
  • Firm information — history, investment philosophy & process, people, press releases, job postings, and contact details
  • Resources — tools, educational materials, and fund literature

Investor type & location

Before they can access any content, visitors are usually asked to select their investor type and/or location from a list of options. Because these selections can’t be verified, lengthy disclaimers are typically included and must be accepted before continuing.

While this can be a rather blunt and off-putting experience — and should therefore be made as seamless as possible — it does allow the website to be personalised for each visitor based on their selections, using rules set in the website content management system (CMS).

A visitor’s selections are typically stored in a cookie that is set on their browser so they don’t have to reselect them each time they visit.

Investor type

For investor type, the simplest option is to offer two choices:

  • Professional investor
  • Non-professional (or retail) investor

Although this does assume all visitors are investors of some kind, those visiting for a different reason — mainly job seekers and journalists — can be grouped under non-professionals. Their access can be restricted to job applications and high-level firm information, while professionals are able to access all content and subscribe to topics of interest.

The professional investor option is commonly divided further into the two client groups — institutional and intermediary — either for familiarity reasons or because the investment manager produces content specifically aimed at one group (e.g. educational content for advisors to use with their retail clients).

Visitor location

When an investment manager offers products in more than one country, website visitors are usually asked to set their location so available pooled funds — and in some cases specific content — can be displayed.

Single vs multiple website domains

If an investment manager offers products in multiple countries, they may choose to host websites on local country domains where products are available (for example, .com, .it, or co.uk) or use a single domain for all visitors. Once a visitor’s location has been selected, they can either be redirected to the relevant domain or content tailored appropriately.

While there are probably SEO benefits from hosting local websites, it can create some complications for smaller digital marketing teams in their day to day work. For example, while a website CMS usually allows a page to be published across multiple domains at the same time, when it comes to distributing content via social media and email, it can be helpful to only have to share a single URL across a global audience.

Languages

Some investment managers make their website available in multiple languages, which can be particularly taxing for the marketing team

Luckily, advances in technology are making the task much easier. Tools are available that can automate translation entirely (such as Weglot) which allow manual corrections or translation rules to be added for specific terms, so they don’t need to be constantly corrected.

Personalisation

Beyond personalising the website for various investor types and locations, there are often aspirations to expand it to include a visitor’s previous browsing behaviour, subscriptions, or even data within their CRM record (like products they might be invested in).

However, it’s important to understand that visits to B2B investment manager’s websites tend to be brief. Unlike Netflix or Amazon — where people land on the homepage with a specific intention to browse for something to watch or buy — visitors usually land on a specific page, find what they are looking for, and leave.

When a visitor does land on the homepage, it’s likely they are looking for something specific, which they want to find quickly. Therefore, it should be a priority to ensure that visitors can find what they need as quickly and easily as possible – through a clear website structure, intuitive navigation, well-presented information, and a useful search function.

There’s no harm in personalising for content preferences — I just wouldn’t expect any earth-shattering results from doing so.
